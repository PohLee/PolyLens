# Project Post-Mortem Playbook

Use this playbook when a project has concluded — successfully or not — to capture lessons learned, celebrate wins, identify improvements, and prevent repeating mistakes on future projects.

## When to Use

- A project has shipped, been cancelled, or reached a major milestone
- You need a structured retrospective beyond "what went well / what went wrong"
- You want to build institutional knowledge from project experience
- You need to identify process improvements for future projects

## Process

### 1. Set the Context

- What was the project and its original objectives?
- What was the planned timeline, budget, and scope?
- What was the actual timeline, budget, and scope?
- Who were the key stakeholders and team members?
- What is the current status? (shipped, cancelled, on hold, pivoted)

### 2. Measure Outcomes Against Intentions

Compare planned vs. actual:

- Did we achieve the original objectives? (fully, partially, not at all)
- What was the variance in timeline? (early, on time, late — by how much)
- What was the variance in budget? (under, on, over — by how much)
- What was the variance in scope? (reduced, unchanged, expanded)
- What was the quality of the outcome? (meets requirements, exceeds, falls short)
- What was the customer or user impact? (adoption, satisfaction, business value)

### 3. Gather Perspectives

Collect input from multiple viewpoints:

- Project team: what was it like to work on this?
- Stakeholders: did we get what we expected?
- Customers or users: did this solve their problem?
- Supporting teams (ops, support, legal): were they prepared?

Use anonymous surveys for honest feedback if the culture does not support open critique.

### 4. Analyze What Happened

**What went well:**
- Specific practices, decisions, or behaviors that produced good outcomes
- Surprises that worked in our favor
- Things we should do again on future projects

**What went wrong:**
- Specific decisions, assumptions, or events that caused problems
- Surprises that worked against us
- Things we should never do again

**What we learned:**
- New knowledge about our product, customers, or processes
- Assumptions that were wrong
- Capabilities we discovered we have (or lack)

### 5. Identify Root Causes for Major Issues

For the 2-3 most significant problems, apply root cause analysis:
- What was the immediate trigger?
- What were the contributing factors?
- What was the underlying systemic cause?
- What could have prevented or detected this earlier?

### 6. Define Action Items

Convert learnings into specific, owned actions:

- Process changes: what should we start, stop, or continue doing?
- Tooling improvements: what tools or automation would help?
- Communication improvements: what information flow needs fixing?
- Skill gaps: what training or hiring is needed?
- Template or playbook updates: what should be documented for reuse?

Each action needs an owner and a deadline.

## Output Pattern

```text
PROJECT OVERVIEW
- Objectives, planned vs. actual timeline/budget/scope, outcome

OUTCOMES VS. INTENTIONS
- What we achieved vs. what we planned

WHAT WENT WELL
- Practices and decisions to repeat

WHAT WENT WRONG
- Problems and decisions to avoid

KEY LEARNINGS
- New knowledge, wrong assumptions, capability gaps

ROOT CAUSES (top 2-3 issues)
- Trigger, contributors, systemic cause, prevention

ACTION ITEMS
- Specific changes with owners and deadlines
```
